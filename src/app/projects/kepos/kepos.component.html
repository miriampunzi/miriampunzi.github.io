K’epos is a cultural association based in Bergamo that promotes classical culture through language courses, readings, and cultural events. Despite its active community, K’epos struggled to engage new audiences and encourage participation beyond Bergamo. Their website, meant to serve as the main information hub, had become difficult to navigate and underused by both members and visitors.

My goal was to redesign the website to make it more accessible, visually appealing, and functional, improving both usability and visibility.

I interviewed the event organizers and website managers to explore:

  • Common questions or issues from participants
  • Pain points in managing and updating content
  • Their goals for the website
01

User Understanding

  • Who do you think are the main users of the website? (age, location, interests)
  • What is the main purpose for which users visit the site?
  • Do you receive any feedback from users? If yes, what are the most common comments?
  • How many people use the website, and how many attend your events?
  • How do people usually find out about your website?
02

Usability Issues

  • Have you noticed any navigation difficulties or confusion from users?
  • In your opinion, how could the events be better organized (e.g., by type, date, or audience)?
  • Has there ever been confusion regarding the date, location, or cost of an event?
  • Are there any sections of the site that you consider underused or unclear?
03

Content Management

  • Do you have any internal guidelines for how events should be presented (e.g., title, date, description)?
  • Have you noticed that some events perform better online than others? If so, what do you think that depends on?
04

Goals

  • What is the main goal of the website, in your opinion? Has it changed over time?
  • If there were no constraints, what would you like to improve about the website?
  • Visitors are mainly adults and retirees interested in courses and readings.
  • Most discover K’epos through social media or word of mouth, then visit the site for more detailed information.
  • The site acts more as a reference point than a discovery channel. Participants goes there to access extra information or fill satisfacion surveys after events.
  • The website follows a chronological blog structure, which makes it hard for organizers to find or update past events. They wish to organize content by type (courses, readings, trips) and include educational materials.
  • The main goal of the association is to increase visibility and attract new audiences outside the Bergamo area. Internally, the website should remain a practical tool for managing registrations and information.

A short Google Forms survey was sent to K’epos event attendees to better understand their habits and needs.

  • Most respondents live in or near Bergamo and discovered the association through social media or school networks.
  • Users mainly visit the website to learn more about activities or to register for courses.
  • Many found event information “a bit disorganized” or “hard to browse.”

Key improvements included:
  • Organized content by event type (e.g., Theater Outings, Readings, Language Courses) with subcategories by year.
  • For recurring event types (e.g., courses), displayed only the most recent poster and current-year details, with links to previous years’ information.
  • Created independent educational content (e.g., short videos on mythology or cultural topics) and highlighted existing materials such as recorded readings.
  • Enhanced underused but engaging sections like “What They Say About Us.”

Design choices to mention:
  • The homepage features an image from the Viridarium of Livia fresco, housed in the National Roman Museum. The choice reflects the association’s name, "K’epos," derived from two Greek words: epos (epic tale, heroic narrative) and kepos (garden). The name can be interpreted as "the garden of the epic" or "the garden of ancient words."
  • Light background colors were used to ensure strong contrast with dark text, improving readability and visual clarity.
  • The primary color, a blue-green tone taken directly from the fresco, is used for buttons, titles, and icons to create a cohesive identity.
  • Each section — Readings, Trips, Courses, and Contests — was assigned a distinct accent color to make navigation and recognition easier.
  • Event posters, which are visually rich and well-designed, were given generous space on the page. At the same time, users can access a more structured view of each event’s details for quick consultation.
